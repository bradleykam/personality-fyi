Signs you might be mistyped on MBTI

Question · Updated 2026-05-17 · By Brad Kam

The short answer

Roughly half of online MBTI test-takers initially mistype on at least one axis. The most common mistypes: INFP scoring as INFJ, ISFJ scoring as INFJ, INTJ scoring as INFJ during grief or burnout, ENFP scoring as ENFJ, and ISTJ scoring as INTJ in mid-career.

Sign 1: Your type description feels generically positive

If your type description feels flattering but vague, you might be looking at the wrong type. The right type description should include uncomfortable parts that feel painfully accurate. If nothing about your type's shadow patterns hits home, that's a flag — your real type's shadow would be visible to you.

Sign 2: People close to you disagree with your type

Self-perception is uniquely unreliable on personality. People who know you well — especially across multiple contexts — see your patterns more clearly than you do. If your spouse and your closest friends both think you're a different type than your test says, take that seriously.

Sign 3: Your type changes when you re-test

Stable types don't change between tests. If you get a different result on a retake, at least one axis is borderline. The axis that flips is usually the one where you sit near the midpoint — your real preference might be the one that came out in a calmer life period, not under stress.

Sign 4: You identify with a 'rare' type

INFJ in particular is over-claimed because the description is unusually flattering. If you scored INFJ, double-check by reading INFP and INTJ descriptions in full — and pay attention to whether their shadow patterns feel uncomfortably accurate. Many INFPs and INTJs are mistyped INFJs because the INFJ description matches what they aspire to.

How to type yourself accurately

Read full descriptions of types similar to yours (single-letter variations). Identify the one whose shadow patterns feel most uncomfortably accurate. Ask 2-3 people who know you in different contexts to suggest a type without seeing yours. Look for the convergence between their guesses and your test result.
